Public Brief

A practical path for languages and their written varieties

Romansh and Ladin are small European language communities with strong internal variation. Sutsilvan is especially underrepresented, with roughly 1,000 active speakers, and conversational fine-tuning material remains scarce across these languages.

RhaetoChat treats this as an infrastructure problem: collect consistent conversational prompts, keep each variety separate, compensate native contributors, and document the limits of the resulting material.

Starting gap Romansh has very limited human-written question-answer material, and Ladin has even less comparable conversational data.
Collection model Machine translation creates a draft; native post-editors turn it into idiomatic Romansh or Ladin.
Research value The same workflow supports LLM adaptation, machine translation, post-editing research, and evaluation.

Project Plan

From safe prompts to reviewed language work

RhaetoChat starts from prompts that have already passed safety review, translates them into Romansh and Ladin varieties, and asks native speakers to post-edit prompts and rank model responses. LITRA keeps the workflow auditable from import to export.

Human review Paid native post-editors and annotators check translations and preference rankings.
Quality and ethics Only prompts marked safe are used, with manual screening before assignment.
Reusable exports LITRA prepares structured JSONL, TXT, and DOCX packages for archiving, analysis, and controlled sharing.
Community partners Lia Rumantscha, unibz, UZH, and the University of Innsbruck connect research with language expertise.

Safeguards

Designed around people, not scraped volume

RhaetoChat centers paid native post-editors and annotators, early recruitment through community networks, contributor agreements, pseudonymous identifiers, and expert feedback on translation samples.

  • Safe prompts are screened before assignment.
  • Annotators work on the variety they know.
  • Known limitations and demographic bias are documented.
  • Project exports omit account names by default.

Milestones

A staged collection plan

The work moves through setup, collection, and reporting phases so recruitment, guidelines, annotation, and quality assurance can advance in a controlled sequence.

  1. Months 1-3 Prepare

    Select prompts, write guidelines, set up the annotation platform, build translation and response pipelines, and recruit native contributors.

  2. Months 4-10 Collect

    Post-edit translations, rank model responses, collect comments, and monitor quality across varieties.

  3. Months 11-12 Report

    Document the workflow, technical findings, limitations, and lessons for future low-resource language projects.
